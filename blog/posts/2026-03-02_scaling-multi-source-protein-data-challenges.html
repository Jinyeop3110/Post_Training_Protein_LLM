The UniProt Convergence Problem: Why Scaling Multi-Source Protein Data Is Harder Than It Looks

2026-03-02 · yeopjin
dataevaluationsft

We assembled a 4.7M-record combined SFT dataset from six independent sources, expecting that more data from more places would mean better coverage of the protein space. The reality is more complicated. A systematic audit revealed two problems that anyone scaling protein instruction data will hit: nearly all sources converge on the same ~440K proteins from UniProt, and evaluation benchmarks share those same proteins — creating contamination that silently inflates metrics.

This post documents what we found, the numbers behind it, and what we're doing about it.

The headline: 4.52 million records, but only 603,334 unique protein sequences. A 7.5x ratio of records to unique proteins. That ratio is not necessarily bad — different tasks on the same protein teach different aspects of protein biology — but the pattern of overlap tells a deeper story.

Challenge 1: The UniProt Convergence Problem

Five of our six sources ultimately derive from the same upstream database: UniProt/Swiss-Prot. ProtDescribe, ProteinLM, SwissProt, and SwissProtCLAP share 95–100% of their protein sequences with each other. Mol-Instructions overlaps ~50% with the UniProt-derived sets. Only Protein2Text-QA (1,265 unique proteins) brings a meaningfully different protein population — and it's the smallest source by far.

The numbers are striking. ProtDescribe shares 99.8% of its proteins with SwissProt. ProteinLM shares 99.9%. These are not independent datasets — they are different annotation layers on top of the same ~440K core proteins from the SwissProt reviewed subset of UniProt.

73.9% of unique proteins appear in 2 or more sources. The dominant bucket is 4 sources (44.0%) — nearly half of all unique sequences appear in exactly four of the six sources. The model will see these proteins repeatedly under different task framings: function description in one source, subcellular location in another, gene name in a third, similarity analysis in a fourth.

The 26.1% that appear in only one source come almost entirely from Mol-Instructions (which includes enzymes from PDB not in SwissProt's reviewed set) and the long tail of SwissProt entries not picked up by other sources. The 286 proteins appearing in all six sources are well-characterized model organisms (calmodulin, ubiquitin, cytochrome c) that collectively generate 222 training records for the most-repeated protein alone.

What This Means for Training

The good news: the 1.19x effective redundancy (counting unique protein-task pairs vs total records) means we're mostly not wasting compute on exact duplicates. Different sources genuinely ask different questions about the same proteins.

The concern: the model's protein "vocabulary" is narrower than the record count suggests. We have 4.52M records but they cover 603K proteins, of which ~440K form a shared core. Proteins outside SwissProt's reviewed set — novel proteins, engineered variants, disordered proteins — are severely underrepresented. Scaling up records from more UniProt-derived sources won't fix this without adding genuinely new protein sequences.

Challenge 2: Evaluation Benchmark Contamination

The convergence problem has a dangerous corollary: if training data and evaluation benchmarks both derive from UniProt, the proteins overlap. We checked every eval benchmark against the combined training set using exact sequence matching.

Critical finding: ProteinLMBench has 98–100% protein overlap with training data — the exact same files appear in both. Any evaluation on ProteinLMBench measures memorization, not generalization.

ProteinLMBench: Direct Data Leakage

The six plm_*.json files in our training set (disease, functionality, induction, ptm, subunit, tissue_specificity) are the same data used as the ProteinLMBench evaluation benchmark. The training files at data/processed/proteinlm/ are byte-for-byte identical to the eval source. This is not a protein-level overlap — it is complete data leakage. Reporting any ProteinLMBench metric for a model trained on this data would be meaningless.

CAFA5 and Structure Quality: Shared Protein Identities

The 55% and 61% overlaps for CAFA5 and Structure Quality are different in character. The model has not seen the same task labels during training — GO terms are not in the SFT data, and structure quality scores are not either. But it has seen 5,524 of the 10,021 CAFA5 proteins described across multiple training tasks (function, naming, location, etc.). The model may have learned protein-specific representations that shortcut the evaluation task.

The training source breakdown for CAFA5 overlap shows the contamination is broad, not concentrated in one file:

MegaScale: The Clean Benchmark

MegaScale Stability is the only benchmark with zero contamination. Its 9,857 unique proteins are synthetic/engineered variants (point mutations of designed proteins) that do not appear in SwissProt. This makes MegaScale the only benchmark where we can trust absolute metric values without decontamination.

What We're Doing About It

Immediate Actions

  1. ProteinLMBench is disqualified as an evaluation benchmark for models trained on the combined dataset. We will either create a proper held-out split from the ProteinLM data (train 80% / eval 20% with protein-level splitting), or drop it from evaluation entirely.
  2. Decontaminated evaluation for CAFA5 and Structure Quality: we will report metrics separately for seen proteins (those in training) and unseen proteins (those not in training). Only unseen-protein metrics reflect true generalization.
  3. MegaScale Stability remains the primary reliable benchmark.

Future Data Improvements

  1. Protein-level train/eval splitting: rather than random record splitting, split by protein identity. No protein should appear in both train and eval, regardless of task.
  2. Sequence clustering for harder eval: use MMseqs2 at 30% sequence identity to create eval sets from entirely different protein families than training. This tests structural generalization, not just sequence memorization.
  3. Non-UniProt protein sources: add data from TrEMBL (unreviewed UniProt), PDB experimental structures, AlphaFold DB predictions, and de novo designed proteins to break out of the SwissProt echo chamber.

Takeaway

The protein instruction data landscape has a bottleneck that record counts obscure: nearly all curated datasets derive from the same ~440K reviewed SwissProt proteins. Scaling from 500K to 4.7M records increases task diversity per protein (which is valuable) but barely expands protein diversity (which is what evaluation needs). Any evaluation benchmark built on UniProt proteins will show contamination with any training set built on UniProt proteins.

For our three-way comparison (text vs MLP vs Perceiver), this means:

  • MegaScale stability is the only fully trustworthy benchmark
  • CAFA5 and Structure Quality metrics must be reported with decontamination splits
  • ProteinLMBench cannot be used at all

The training run on the combined dataset launched today (sft_esm3_mlp_combined_qwen3_8b_it_0302_125834). We proceed with the data as-is, since the contamination is an evaluation problem, not a training problem. The model will still benefit from seeing diverse tasks on familiar proteins. We just need to be honest about what our benchmarks measure.
